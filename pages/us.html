Justice Department quietly closes federal inmate death inquiry, citing lack of evidence of foul play.

The closure, announced in a one-paragraph press release late Friday afternoon, has drawn swift criticism from civil liberties groups and at least two sitting senators.
By Lena Forsyth | Updated 3 hours ago
The Department of Justice announced Friday that it would not be pursuing further investigation into the death of a federal inmate whose case drew national attention earlier this year. The closure of case no. 7731-A was communicated via a brief statement issued at 4:52 p.m., a time critics noted was "calculated to minimize press coverage going into the weekend." The statement offered no new findings, no forensic conclusions, and no comment from the medical examiner whose preliminary report has never been made public.

Case closed, questions open: the federal inmate death inquiry that ended with a paragraph

By Lena Forsyth | Washington, D.C. | Updated 3 hours ago

The decision to close case no. 7731-A came without warning to at least three Congressional offices who had been monitoring the inquiry's progress, according to staffers who spoke on the condition of anonymity. "We were told last week there would be a briefing," said one staffer. "Instead we got the same press release everyone else got, forty minutes before close of business."

The case involved the death of a federal detainee who had been held at the Metropolitan Correctional Center in Houston pending trial on what the DOJ described at the time as "digital compliance violations." The circumstances of the death were disputed from the outset. The official finding of suicide was challenged by two independent forensic consultants retained by the deceased's legal team, neither of whom have been permitted to review the full autopsy report.

Attorney General Nathan Bartholemew, who earlier this year described the case as raising "serious questions that must be answered," did not issue a personal statement alongside Friday's closure announcement. A spokesperson for his office said he was "traveling" and would "have more to say next week." He has made no further public comment on the matter as of press time.

The Bureau of Prisons declined to comment on the closure. The FBI, which had announced it was opening a parallel inquiry in August, confirmed only that its review had been "concluded in coordination with DOJ." It did not say what that review found, when it was completed, or who authorized the coordination.

Among those calling for the records to be released is Patrick O'mallaghathy, a former colleague of the deceased, who has maintained since the original announcement that the official account is inconsistent with what he knows of the timeline. "There are things in those files that don't match what we were told," O'mallaghathy said in a statement posted Thursday, the day before the closure was announced. "Someone knew this was coming."
