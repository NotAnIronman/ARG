$340,000, no source, no explanation: inside the Deli Aisle Network's unexplained finances

By Voss Harrity | Fort Worth, Texas | Updated 4 hours ago

Financial disclosures filed as part of a federal asset review are rarely interesting. Document 44-B is an exception. Across eleven pages of otherwise standard accounting, a single line item appears with no supporting documentation, no category description, and no reference number: "Operational Continuity — $340,000." Three forensic accountants reviewed the document at the request of Deli Aisle News. All three said the same thing in different words: this entry does not conform to any standard accounting category they are familiar with.

"It's not a legal term, it's not an accounting term, and it's not a tax category," said one, who asked to remain anonymous due to ongoing work with federal clients. "It's a phrase someone invented. The question is why they needed to invent it." The remaining $72,000 in disclosed revenues is accounted for through documented membership subscriptions and what the filing describes as "community partnership arrangements" — a category that itself raises questions but at least exists as a concept.

The Deli Aisle Network's current leadership, under acting administrator Gustau, did not respond to a request for comment on the $340,000 figure. A representative acknowledged receipt of the inquiry and said a response would be provided "when appropriate." That was eleven days ago. Freedough, who had been considered a candidate for the leadership role before T Wing's appointment, told Deli Aisle News he had "no knowledge of any revenue stream that wasn't fully disclosed to the full membership." When shown the 44-B figure, he was quiet for several seconds. "That's not a number I recognize," he said.

What the Deli Aisle Network was doing with $412,000 in annual revenue — for an organization whose stated purpose was coordinating daily Wordle completions and facilitating community discussion — remains an open question. August's legal team, in filings made before his death, argued that the revenue figures were "mischaracterized" by federal prosecutors. They did not offer an alternative characterization. The judge in the case noted this absence in a footnote on page 34 of the pretrial ruling. That footnote reads, in full: "The Court notes that the defendant has not offered documentation to support, contradict, or contextualize the financial figures submitted under seal. This absence is noted for the record."
