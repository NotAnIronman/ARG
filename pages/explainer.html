Emergency alert systems: the infrastructure of public information, and who has the keys

By Aldous Prenn | Fort Worth, Texas

The Wireless Emergency Alert system — the one that makes your phone buzz and scream in the middle of the night — is not run by a single neutral authority. It is a patchwork of federal agencies, telecommunications carriers, and local governments, each with partial control over different aspects of what gets sent, when, and to whom.

At the federal level, FEMA and the FCC share oversight of the system's national infrastructure. But the decision to send a specific alert — a missing child, an approaching tornado, an imminent threat — is made at the state or local level, by an authorized operator who has been granted credentials to use the system. Those credentials are issued by the state emergency management agency, which receives them from the federal government.

What this means in practice: a relatively small number of people have the ability to push a message to every phone in a given geographic area. And a similarly small number of people have the ability to decide that a particular message should not be sent.

The system has no public log of messages that were drafted but not sent. It has no public record of alerts that were delayed. It has no mechanism by which the public can determine whether an authorized operator chose not to send a warning they were authorized to send. The infrastructure of emergency communication, in other words, is also the infrastructure of its potential suppression.

Most of the time, none of this matters. Most of the time, the system works as intended and the people with the keys use them responsibly. But understanding who holds those keys — and what they can choose not to do with them — is part of understanding how public information actually flows in a crisis.
