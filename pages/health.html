Former MCC Houston staff member describes "deteriorating" conditions in August's unit weeks before his death — and a request that was denied.

The account, provided to Deli Aisle News on the condition of anonymity, describes a man who was "frightened, not depressed" — and who made a formal request to be moved to a different housing unit that was denied without explanation.
By Prynne Delacourt | Updated 7 hours ago
In the weeks before August's death, he made a formal written request to be relocated to a different housing unit within MCC Houston, according to a former facility staff member who spoke to Deli Aisle News. The request, submitted approximately ten days before he was found dead, was denied. The denial came without a stated reason. It was processed and returned to August within 48 hours — faster, the former staff member noted, than such requests are typically handled. "When something moves that fast, it usually means someone above made a call," they said.

"He wasn't depressed. He was scared." A former MCC Houston staff member speaks.

By Prynne Delacourt | Fort Worth, Texas | Updated 7 hours ago

The former staff member, who left MCC Houston several months before August's death and asked not to be identified by name, department, or gender, described the housing unit where August was held as "not a place where someone in his state of mind should have been." They said the unit had a history of maintenance issues, staffing shortages, and what they described as "an environment that was stressful for everyone in it, staff included."

August, they said, had been "visibly anxious" in the weeks before his death — not clinically depressed, not expressing suicidal ideation that the source was aware of, but "frightened in a way that felt different from the other inmates I'd seen go through difficult stretches." The source said August had asked multiple staff members about the transfer request's status in the days after it was denied. "He kept asking. Not aggressively. Just — persistently. Like it mattered a lot."

The denial itself is the part the source found most unusual. Transfer requests at MCC Houston typically take seven to fourteen days to process, they said. August's came back in 48 hours — a turnaround they described as "almost unheard of for a non-emergency request." The denial form, they said, listed no reason in the space provided for one. That space is required to be filled in under BOP policy. It was not.

The Bureau of Prisons did not respond to questions about the transfer request, the timeline of its denial, or the blank reason field. A spokesperson said in a general statement that "all requests submitted by inmates in federal custody are handled in accordance with established Bureau policy." When asked whether a 48-hour turnaround with no stated reason conformed to that policy, the spokesperson did not reply.

Mental health advocates who reviewed the account at Deli Aisle News's request said the picture it painted was "inconsistent with a man in acute suicidal crisis" — and more consistent, they said, with "someone who believed they were in danger from an external source and was trying to do something about it." They cautioned that any such assessment based on secondhand account is limited. "But the question of why that request was denied so fast, with no explanation, is a legitimate one," said one advocate. "Someone should answer it."
