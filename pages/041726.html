Local man reports "suspicious" goose activity near Tarpon Springs courthouse; demands federal inquiry.

Gerald Pimms, 54, says the geese have been "watching him specifically" since the trial began. Ornithologists remain unconvinced.
By Cass Hullery | Updated 22 minutes ago
A Tarpon Springs man filed a formal complaint with the Pinellas County Sheriff's Office on Thursday, alleging that a group of Canada geese stationed outside the downtown courthouse had been "coordinating surveillance" on his movements for the better part of six weeks. Gerald Pimms, a retired vending machine technician, described the geese as "unusually still" and said one in particular had been making direct eye contact with him every morning since August 14th. "They don't move like regular geese," Pimms told reporters outside the sheriff's office. "They're waiting for something."

Tarpon Springs man insists courthouse geese are "definitely not normal geese"

By Cass Hullery | Tarpon Springs, Florida | Updated 22 minutes ago

Gerald Pimms has submitted a 14-page handwritten document to the sheriff's office outlining what he calls "Phase One of a coordinated avian perimeter operation." The document includes annotated photographs, several of which appear to be motion-blurred images of a parking lot, and one that Pimms identifies as "the ringleader" — a goose that experts confirmed is, in fact, a goose.

"I've done my research," said Pimms, who cited three YouTube videos and a forum post from 2009. "These animals are capable of memory retention. They remember faces. And mine is a face they have chosen to remember." Dr. Lorraine Thetch, an ornithologist at the University of South Florida, reviewed Pimms' photographs at the request of Deli Aisle News. "They're geese," she said. "They stand near water. They look at things. That's what geese do."

Pimms rejected this assessment. He noted that Dr. Thetch had not personally visited the courthouse lawn, and therefore could not speak to "the energy." He has requested that the FBI, the FAA, and what he described as "whoever handles birds federally" launch independent investigations.

The Pinellas County Sheriff's Office confirmed receipt of the complaint and said it would be reviewed "in accordance with standard procedure," which a spokesperson clarified meant it would be placed in a filing cabinet. As of press time, the geese had not responded to requests for comment. One was observed eating a piece of bread near the fountain.

The incident has drawn mild attention online, where a post about Pimms' complaint received 847 likes on a local Facebook group before being moved to a thread titled "Things That Are Not Crimes." Pimms says the online response proves "people know something is off." He has announced plans to return to the courthouse lawn Monday with a lawn chair, a thermos, and what he described as "a notebook for documentation purposes."
