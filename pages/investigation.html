The anatomy of a federal case: who watches, who decides, and who can make it go away

By Prynne Delacourt | Fort Worth, Texas

Federal investigations into private organizations rarely begin with a tip, a warrant, or a smoking gun. They begin, more often, with an interest. Someone in a U.S. Attorney's office — or someone with influence over a U.S. Attorney's office — decides that a particular organization merits attention. From that decision, an entire machinery can be set in motion. Or not. The decision belongs, almost entirely, to a single individual.

"The U.S. Attorney has enormous discretionary power," said one former federal prosecutor who asked not to be named. "They can open a case. They can close a case. They can keep a case open indefinitely without ever bringing charges. And in most instances, there is no external check on those decisions."

That discretion extends to what gets sealed and what gets published. Indictment supplements — documents that accompany a formal charge but are not released to the public — can contain identifying information about co-conspirators, financial arrangements, or prior relationships that the charging U.S. Attorney chooses not to disclose. The decision about what goes into the sealed supplement, and what stays out, belongs to the same office that filed the charge.

Former prosecutors describe this arrangement as necessary for protecting ongoing investigations and sensitive sources. Critics describe it as a system in which the most powerful person in a given case can shape what the public ever knows about it. "You are trusting the institution to police itself," said one legal scholar. "When the institution has a conflict of interest, that trust is misplaced."

The process for closing a case is even less transparent. A U.S. Attorney can decline to pursue charges, withdraw an indictment, or — as in the case of a defendant who dies in custody — allow the matter to dissolve without ever formally accounting for why certain counts were filed, what evidence supported them, or who else may have been implicated. The record exists. It simply doesn't have to be made public.
