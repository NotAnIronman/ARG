Inside the investigation: What authorities are looking for — and what they aren’t saying

By Rylet Crash | Houston, Texas | Updated 14 minutes ago

According to officials familiar with the inquiry, investigators are focusing on three key areas: the timeline of August’s final days, the integrity of the Deli Aisle’s internal records, and the circumstances surrounding the hacking attempts on the Deli Aisle News website early Wednesday morning.

Attorney General Nathan Barth has repeatedly emphasized that “all proper procedures were followed” and that the department is “cooperating fully with oversight bodies.” Barth has not addressed questions about why August’s request for archived transaction logs — submitted just hours before his arrest — was never fulfilled.

Richard Saint, the Manhattan-based attorney who originally pushed for the Wordle evasion charges, maintains that the case was “open and shut.” When asked about the financial irregularities referenced in the unsealed filings, Saint said those details were “outside the scope” of his involvement.

But internal documents reviewed by Deli Aisle News show that investigators flagged a series of “miscellaneous adjustments” in the Deli Aisle’s financial system dating back several years. The adjustments, often for amounts under $20, appear to have been routed through a third-party vendor whose listed address corresponds to a vacant office suite in downtown Rosenberg.

Patricio Olivarez, a former coworker who worked closely with August, said the adjustments were “not normal” and that he had raised concerns months earlier. “I told him something was off,” Olivarez said. “He said he’d look into it. Then everything happened so fast.”

Enrique Holst, a longtime friend of August, said he had no knowledge of the financial discrepancies but confirmed that August had been “stressed” in the days leading up to his arrest. “He kept saying he was trying to fix something,” Holst said. “He didn’t tell me what.”

Investigators are also examining the hacking attempts on the Deli Aisle News website, which occurred within minutes of August being discovered unresponsive. Officials have not confirmed whether the two events are connected, but cybersecurity analysts consulted by Deli Aisle News noted that the intrusion targeted archived financial PDFs previously published as part of a 2019 feature on the Deli Aisle’s rapid growth.

Two individuals present near the federal facility on Wednesday morning — identified as Theodore Wing and Fred Dough — gave conflicting accounts of what they saw. Wing claimed he saw “a group of people in suits” entering through a side entrance, while Dough insisted the facility was “completely quiet.” Neither account has been corroborated.

Despite the growing list of unanswered questions, officials maintain that there is “no evidence of foul play.” The FBI has opened an inquiry “out of an abundance of caution,” though a spokesperson emphasized that such reviews are “standard in high-profile cases.”

For now, the investigation continues — but with missing records, contradictory statements, and a timeline that appears to shift with each new briefing, the full picture remains elusive.
