National Wordle Championships rocked by multi-year cheating conspiracy; sources allege "entire eastern bracket was compromised."

A 200-page leaked report details alleged timestamp manipulation, pre-shared word lists, and one competitor who sources claim "had never once played legitimately."
By Prynne Delacourt | Updated 47 minutes ago
The Wordle integrity crisis deepened Thursday as a leaked internal report obtained by Deli Aisle News revealed that as many as 34 competitors in the 2024 National Wordle Championships may have had access to that day's word before the clock struck midnight. The report, compiled by an independent auditing firm hired by the National Word Game Oversight Council (NWGOC), alleges a scheme spanning at least 19 months in which competitors allegedly used a private messaging channel to share "hints, partials, and in some cases the full word" with one another. "The integrity of the green tile has been compromised," the report states on page one.

"The green tile has been compromised": inside the Wordle cheating ring that shook competitive word-gaming

By Prynne Delacourt | Fort Worth, Texas | Updated 47 minutes ago

The group chat, according to the report, operated under the name "CRANE gang" — a reference to the popular opening word used by many competitive players. Membership fluctuated between 12 and 31 individuals over the course of the scheme, and messages were allegedly deleted on a rolling 24-hour cycle to avoid detection. "They were sophisticated," said one NWGOC investigator who spoke on the condition of anonymity. "They had a whole system. Color-coded warnings. A designated person who sourced the word each night. They called him the Extractor."

The Extractor's identity has not been confirmed, though the report narrows the suspects to a pool of four individuals based on timezone data and posting patterns. All four have denied involvement. One, reached by phone, said only "I use ADIEU" before ending the call. The NWGOC suspended competition pending the outcome of a formal review, which is expected to conclude sometime in the next three to six months, or possibly longer, depending on what one official described as "how complicated this gets."

Senator Marlowe Pitts (R-NE), who chairs the Senate Subcommittee on Recreational Digital Integrity, called the scandal "a wake-up call for this nation's casual gaming infrastructure" and announced plans to hold hearings in the fall. "If we cannot trust the Wordle, what can we trust?" Pitts said in a statement released Thursday afternoon.

The New York Times, which owns and operates Wordle, issued a brief statement saying it was "aware of the allegations" and had "full confidence in the integrity of its daily puzzle," adding that the answer selection process involved "multiple layers of review that have not been compromised." Competitive players, however, are not reassured. Several former champions have called for all results from the 2023-2024 season to be vacated. One, posting to a Discord server with over 40,000 members, wrote simply: "Everything is yellow now."

The scandal has also reignited debate over whether competitive Wordle should be subject to independent oversight at all. Proponents argue that the game's simplicity makes cheating trivially easy and therefore demands external enforcement. Critics counter that it is a free browser game and perhaps this level of institutional scrutiny is disproportionate. "We're talking about a five-letter word," said Dr. Heathfield Bonnar, a sports ethics professor at the University of Nebraska. "And yet here we are."
