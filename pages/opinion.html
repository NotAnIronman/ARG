Opinion: Why this moment feels different — and why the people who want it to go away know it too.

A contributing writer who asks to remain anonymous explains why the August case has not, and will not, disappear quietly.
By A Contributing Writer | Opinion
Opinion
Share: 🔗🐦📘

They keep trying to close this. It keeps not closing.

By A Contributing Writer | The views expressed are the author's own.

I have watched a lot of stories get buried. I know what it looks like when someone with institutional power decides that a thing should stop being a thing, and then applies that power consistently and patiently until it stops. Press conferences at 4 p.m. on Fridays. Statements that don't answer questions. Transcripts that are shorter than the recordings.

This one isn't closing. And I think the people trying to close it know that.

The reason isn't that the press has been unusually dogged, or that the public is unusually attentive, or that justice has some mystical tendency toward self-correction. The reason is simpler: someone on the inside is not cooperating. Someone who knows what happened is not staying quiet, even when they're asked to. The deleted post. The phone call that ended abruptly. The source who says "I know which floor" and then stops talking.

What's different about this moment is not the crime. Crimes happen. What's different is that the architecture of the cover-up is visible in a way it usually isn't. You can see the seams. The contradictory statements. The transcript that's 15 minutes shorter than the audio. The financial document with a $340,000 line item that nobody can explain. These are not the marks of a sophisticated operation. They are the marks of people who expected this to be easier than it has been.

They expected the press to stop asking. Some did. They expected the public to move on. Most have. They expected the people who knew things to stay quiet. Most have. But not all. And "most" is not "all." In a story like this, "not all" is enough.

August is dead. The case is officially closed. And yet here we are, at 3 a.m., updating a live blog. That's not normal. The people responsible for this being abnormal know who they are. I hope they understand that other people are starting to, too.
